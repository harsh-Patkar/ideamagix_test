Treatment Overview

What is Total Knee Replacement?

Total knee replacement, also known as knee arthroplasty, is a surgical procedure to resurface a knee damaged by arthritis. Metal and plastic parts are used to cap the ends of the bones that form the knee joint, along with the kneecap.

  • Relieves severe knee pain
  • Improves mobility and function
  • Enhances quality of life
  • Long-lasting results

Eligibility for Surgery

Severe Knee Pain

Persistent pain that limits everyday activities like walking, climbing stairs, or getting in and out of chairs.

Moderate to Severe Arthritis

Diagnosed with osteoarthritis, rheumatoid arthritis, or post-traumatic arthritis affecting the knee joint.

Failed Conservative Treatment

Other treatments like medications, physical therapy, or injections have not provided adequate relief.

Good Overall Health

Physically and mentally prepared for surgery and the rehabilitation process that follows.

Preparation for Surgery

1

Medical Evaluation

Complete physical examination and medical history review to ensure you're fit for surgery.

2

Pre-operative Tests

Blood tests, X-rays, ECG, and other necessary diagnostic tests will be conducted.

3

Medication Review

Review current medications and adjust as needed. Some medications may need to be stopped before surgery.

4

Home Preparation

Arrange for help at home and make necessary modifications for easier post-surgery recovery.

Post-Treatment Care

Hospital Stay

Typically 2-3 days with pain management and initial physical therapy.

Wound Care

Keep the surgical site clean and dry. Follow dressing change instructions carefully.

Physical Therapy

Essential for recovery. Begin exercises as directed by your therapist.

Recovery Tips

Week 1-2

Initial Recovery

  • Focus on wound healing and pain management
  • Begin gentle exercises as instructed
  • Use assistive devices for walking
Week 3-6

Progressive Mobility

  • Increase physical therapy intensity
  • Gradually reduce pain medication
  • Resume light daily activities
Month 2-3

Building Strength

  • Continue strengthening exercises
  • Return to most normal activities
  • May resume driving if cleared by doctor
Month 4-6

Full Recovery

  • Most patients achieve full recovery
  • Return to low-impact sports
  • Enjoy improved quality of life
